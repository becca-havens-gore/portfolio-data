Case Study — Observability & Data Visualization

Reducing Time to Insight for Security Engineers

How I designed a unified telemetry visualization system that transformed fragmented signal data into a cohesive, actionable story of platform health — across six enterprise security products.

The Problem: A Sea of Signal, No Path to Insight

Rapid7's platform processes enormous volumes of security telemetry — detections, vulnerability data, log streams, alert sequences, risk scores, and remediation workflows — across six products serving security engineers, SOC analysts, and security leaders.

But each product team had independently built their own dashboards, charts, and reporting tools. The result was a fragmented observability experience where users couldn't build a coherent mental model of what was happening across their environment.

Analysts were drowning in data with no clear path from raw signal to decision. Time-to-insight was measured in hours, not minutes. Moving between products felt like switching tools entirely. The cognitive overhead was substantial — and in security operations, cognitive overhead is an operational risk.

"I need to know what's happening right now — I don't have time to decode visuals that are different in every product."

— Senior Security Analyst, field research

I led the end-to-end design strategy and architecture for the Data Visualization System (DVS) — Rapid7's first unified platform for observability, dashboards, reporting, and drill-in investigation workflows.

The Users: Technical Practitioners Under Pressure

Security operations teams are a particularly demanding design audience. They're highly technical, work with high-volume data streams, operate in high-stakes environments, and have zero tolerance for ambiguity in visualizations. Misread trends cost real hours of investigation — or worse, missed threats.

This makes security engineers and SOC analysts a close parallel to infrastructure engineers working in observability platforms: they both need to move from aggregate signal to root cause quickly, navigate complex dependency relationships, and do it without needing to be an expert in every underlying system.

Security Analyst
"I need to see what's happening right now, understand if it's real, and drill into why — without losing context."
Security Engineer
"I need dashboards that tell me system health at a glance and let me get to the signal fast when something's off."
Security Leader
"I need to understand trends, risk posture, and the story behind the data — in under 60 seconds."

What Research Told Us

  • Context switching between products destroyed mental models. Analysts who worked across InsightIDR (SIEM/detection), InsightVM (vulnerability), and InsightOps (log management) had to completely relearn each dashboard paradigm.
  • Inconsistent visualization led to misinterpretation. Different chart styles for the same metric type (risk scores, trend lines, severity distributions) caused analysts to read data differently depending on which product they were in.
  • Drill-in paths were unpredictable. The path from a summary metric to root-cause investigation was different in every product. Users learned workarounds instead of workflows.
  • Cognitive load peaked at the moment of investigation. The exact moment users most needed clarity — when something appeared wrong — was when the tool became hardest to use.

Constraints That Shaped the Design

This wasn't a greenfield system. The constraints were real and shaped every architecture decision:

Technical Constraints

  • Six existing visualization paradigms to unify — no full rewrite
  • Multiple frontend stacks across product teams
  • High-density data: thousands of data points per dashboard view
  • Performance non-negotiable — real-time data streams, no acceptable lag
  • Charts required accessibility (WCAG AA minimum) at high data density

User & Business Constraints

  • Must serve both executive overview and deep analyst investigation use cases
  • Enterprise reporting requirements (PDF export, print-optimized themes)
  • Existing dashboards couldn't be removed — migration path required
  • Six product teams with different roadmap priorities and timelines
  • Design adoption required without centralized mandate

Strategy & Architecture: Three Interoperable Layers

I designed DVS as a layered system where each layer builds on the one beneath it. This architecture made the system composable — teams could adopt individual layers without needing a full rewrite, and the layers reinforced each other over time.

1
Telemetry Foundations — Tokens, Color Semantics & Chart Grammar
Shared color semantics for severity, risk, and status. Standardized chart grammar: what chart type means what, in what context. Data density rules. Accessibility specifications at high density. These foundations made every layer above them coherent.
2
Card System — Composable Visualization Components
Self-contained, reusable visualization cards: KPI cards, trend/time-series cards, top-N lists, table cards, correlation cards, timeline/sequence cards, and investigation drill-in cards. Each card defined its inputs, interaction model, empty/error states, drill-in behavior, and accessibility rules. These were the atoms of every dashboard.
3
Dashboard & Report Templates
Overview dashboards for at-a-glance platform health. Custom dashboards for personalized investigator views. Cross-product Home experiences. Report templates for PDF export. The template layer made it easy for teams to compose coherent views without re-solving layout, hierarchy, or information architecture from scratch.

The Drill-In Model: From Signal to Story

The most critical design challenge was the investigation workflow — the path from "something looks wrong" to "here's what happened and why." I designed a consistent drill-in model across all card types:

  • Summary → Trend → Detail → Root Cause: Every drill-in path followed a predictable hierarchy. Users always knew where they were and how to go deeper.
  • Context preservation: Drilling in never lost the originating context. Users could always see what surface triggered the investigation.
  • Cross-product linking: Signals from one product could link into investigation workflows in another — e.g., a vulnerability finding in InsightVM linking to detection data in InsightIDR.
  • Progressive disclosure: Aggregate metrics on the surface, details on demand. Never overwhelming the analyst with all the data at once.

"This is the first time I can read dashboards the same way across the platform."

— Senior Security Analyst, post-launch research

Filtering & Query Consistency

A secondary but critical design problem was filtering. Each product had its own filtering logic and UI. I defined a cross-product filtering and query model that standardized:

  • Filter panel placement, behavior, and state persistence
  • Query construction UI for analysts who needed to write complex filters
  • Applied filter indicators visible across all cards on a dashboard
  • Time range selectors unified in behavior and keyboard accessibility

This eliminated the most common source of confusion in cross-product sessions: users not realizing a filter applied in one product wasn't carried over to another.

The Tradeoff We Accepted

No architecture decision is free. The most consequential tradeoff in DVS:

What We Accepted

We deliberately did not create a single unified charting library that all products must adopt simultaneously. Instead, DVS introduced a shared specification layer — shared semantics, card contracts, and interaction patterns — while allowing teams to implement using their existing frontend stack.

Why

A forced migration of all six charting libraries would have stalled adoption for 12+ months. By separating specification from implementation, teams could begin delivering consistent experiences immediately, with library unification as a later optimization. User coherence came first; technical uniformity could follow.

This was a deliberate sequencing decision: optimize for user experience coherence now, even if the underlying implementations aren't yet unified. It meant more coordination overhead short-term, but it let us move six product teams in parallel without blocking on infrastructure.

Working with Engineering

DVS was as much an engineering collaboration as a design effort. I worked directly with platform engineers on:

  • Shared data adapters: Standardized the shape of data flowing into visualization components, reducing the per-product work to implement a new card type.
  • Performance specifications: Defined render budgets for high-density charts — what was acceptable latency for different data volumes, how virtualization should work for large table cards.
  • Accessibility implementation: Chart accessibility at high data density requires specific keyboard navigation models, ARIA patterns, and color contrast specifications. I authored these and worked with engineers to validate them in code.
  • Design token integration: DVS tokens were integrated into the R7 Design System, making visualization color semantics part of the platform-wide token system rather than a parallel specification.

I attended engineering sprint reviews, participated in architecture discussions, and shipped detailed component specifications that engineers described as "implementation-ready." The goal was to make the gap between Figma and production as narrow as possible.

Behavioral Signals That Mattered Most

  • Reduced error escalations: Tickets related to "dashboard misread" or "chart confusion" declined meaningfully in the quarters following DVS adoption.
  • Cross-product navigation increased: Users who previously stayed within a single product began moving across products fluidly — a direct signal that the coherent navigation model was working.
  • Analyst training time reduced: Customer success reported shorter onboarding timelines for new analysts, attributed to the predictable dashboard paradigm.
  • Qualitative feedback shift: Pre-DVS, dashboard feedback in user interviews was dominated by confusion and workaround discussion. Post-DVS, it shifted toward feature requests — users were focused on expanding capability rather than surviving the existing UX.

The Connection to Platform Observability

Security operations platforms and infrastructure observability platforms share the same fundamental design challenge: how do you help technical practitioners navigate massive volumes of signal data to find what matters, understand what it means, and act on it — fast?

The users are different. The domain is different. But the core design problems are structurally identical:

  • Aggregate views that surface anomalies without overwhelming
  • Drill-in paths from summary to root cause that preserve context
  • Consistent telemetry semantics across a fragmented multi-surface platform
  • AI-assisted analysis that reduces cognitive load without removing analyst control
  • Cross-surface coherence so practitioners can move between tools without relearning

DVS taught me that the hardest part of observability design isn't making charts look good — it's making the relationships between signals legible, the paths to action obvious, and the cognitive overhead low enough that people can think clearly when the system is on fire.

That's the problem I want to keep solving.
