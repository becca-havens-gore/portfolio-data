Executive Summary

As AI-powered features matured across Rapid7's platform, teams began delivering summaries, recommendations, predictions, and auto-generated content—but without unified attribution, explainability, trust models, or user control. In a cybersecurity context where accuracy, provenance, and reliability are paramount, inconsistent AI UX posed significant risk.

I led the strategy, architecture, and delivery of the AI Experience Standard, Rapid7's first cross-product framework for responsible enterprise AI. Together with the AI Center of Excellence, Design System team, Platform UX, and product designers, we created principles, patterns, permissions, and explainability models that made AI interactions consistent, trustworthy, and scalable across six product lines.

We also operationalized the standard into the design system—making it consumable by AI tools like Supernova and MCP—enabling system-aware AI copilots that keep product teams aligned, compliant, and efficient.

Context

Rapid7's platform was shipping multiple AI-driven features:

  • Generative summaries of alerts, detections, and investigations
  • Predictive recommendations
  • Automated report generation
  • AI-assisted queries
  • In-context triage suggestions

However, each team implemented AI features differently, leading to:

  • Dramatic variation in explainability
  • No standard for confidence indicators or provenance
  • No consistent AI attribution
  • No user controls or permissions framework
  • No shared "AI design system" for designers or engineers

In cybersecurity, unclear AI behavior can undermine user trust—and trust is non-negotiable.

The Problem

Without a unified standard:

User Impact

  • Users couldn't tell what was human-generated vs. AI-generated
  • Analysts lacked insight into why an AI recommendation was made
  • AI outputs lacked consistent disclaimers or confidence levels
  • Accessibility and clarity varied from team to team
  • Capabilities were shipped without adequate permission controls
  • AI features risked harming trust in high-stakes environments

Internal Team Impact

  • Designers recreated AI UI patterns independently
  • Engineers implemented inconsistent logic
  • No shared documentation or governance existed
  • AI models had no structured design system metadata to draw from

We needed a design system for AI itself—and a governance model to ensure safe, transparent, and responsible AI across the platform.

Research & User Insights

I collaborated with UX Research to analyze 9 customer interviews with Security Analysts, Managers, and Directors, examining SOC workflows and AI touchpoints.

Key Research Insights

1. Trust must be earned, not assumed

Users expect AI output from Rapid7 to be correct by default.

2. Transparency reduces cognitive load

Users want to understand why, how, and based on what data an output was generated.

3. Analysts need provenance

They want drill-downs into the underlying data—especially in incident investigations.

4. Control matters

Users want AI to assist, not automate decisions without oversight.

5. AI must reduce workload, not increase it

Users want fewer steps—not more complex workflows.

6. Privacy and data handling transparency are non-negotiable

Clear communication about data usage and storage is essential.

Strategy & Framework Architecture

The AI Experience Standard includes four foundational pillars:

⭐ Pillar 1: Responsible AI Principles

We established six principles validated with customers and internal stakeholders:

Transparency
Make it clear when AI is used
Trust
Accuracy and provenance matter
Control
Users decide how/when AI is applied
Value
AI must meaningfully reduce work
Simplicity
Avoid cognitive overload
Data Safety
Clear boundaries around usage + storage

Each pattern included usage guidelines, behavioral specs, accessibility requirements, responsive rules, and interaction logic.

⭐ Pillar 3: Permission & Safety Architecture

We defined a multi-layer permissions model:

  • Global-level controls: Admins can disable/enable all AI across all products
  • Product-level controls: Each product can expose its own AI capabilities toggle
  • Workspace-level controls: Fine-grained control for teams with different risk profiles
  • Feature-level controls: Every new AI feature must ship as opt-in, not opt-out

This model was aligned with Legal, Privacy, and the AI Center of Excellence.

⭐ Pillar 4: Design System Integration & AI System Ingestion

I partnered with the RDS team to make AI patterns first-class citizens in the design system, including component specs, token taxonomy, MUI variants, accessibility requirements, and Figma component sets.

Innovation: AI-Aware Design System

We made RDS machine-readable, enabling AI tools to query component metadata using:

  • Supernova design system ingestion
  • MCP (Model Context Protocol) connectors
  • Structured schema for pattern logic
  • Documentation mapping

This allows internal AI assistants to answer questions like "What pattern should I use for an AI recommendation?" or "Show me the explainability drawer spec." This is AI-assisted governance, and it's forward-looking.
