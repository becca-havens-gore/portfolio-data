The Problem: AI That Created Work Instead of Reducing It

Rapid7's platform was shipping AI-powered features at pace: generative alert summaries, predictive recommendations, AI-assisted triage, automated investigation narratives. But each product team built AI interactions differently, with no shared standard for how AI output should be surfaced, attributed, or explained.

For security engineers and SOC analysts — technical practitioners who work with high-stakes data in high-pressure environments — inconsistent AI UX wasn't just annoying. It was a trust problem.

Users couldn't tell what was AI-generated versus human-generated. They didn't know why a recommendation was made, what data it drew from, or how confident the model was. They couldn't control when or how AI was applied. And in a domain where a miscommunicated risk level can mean the difference between a closed alert and a breach, that ambiguity had real consequences.

"I don't know if I'm reading a human-written summary or an AI guess. That matters when I'm deciding whether to escalate."

— Security Analyst, pre-standard research interview

The deeper problem: AI was increasing cognitive load rather than reducing it. Practitioners had to mentally audit every AI output before trusting it, without any consistent signals to guide that audit. The promise of AI — help engineers find the needle in the haystack faster — was being undermined by UX that made the haystack bigger.

Why This Is a Developer Platform Problem Too

The security operations context and infrastructure observability context share the same core AI UX challenge: how do you surface AI-generated insights to technical practitioners in ways that reduce investigation time without requiring them to blindly trust the model?

Whether it's an AI-suggested root cause for a service degradation, an anomaly detection flag on a metrics stream, or an automated alert summary in a SOC tool — the design problems are structurally identical:

  • How do you communicate confidence without creating false certainty?
  • How do you give users provenance — why did the model surface this? — without overwhelming the UI?
  • How do you let users correct or dismiss AI output without friction?
  • How do you make AI genuinely reduce steps rather than add a new thing to audit?

The patterns I built for Rapid7's security engineers are directly applicable to the AI-assisted troubleshooting workflows Netflix is building for infrastructure engineers.

Research: What Technical Users Actually Need from AI

I led research synthesis across 9 customer interviews with security analysts, managers, and directors, examining where AI helped, where it added friction, and what trust actually looked like in practice.

Six Core Insights

  • Trust must be earned through provenance. Technical users don't trust AI output categorically — they trust it when they can see what data it drew from and understand the reasoning chain. "Show your work" wasn't just preferred; it was required for adoption.
  • Transparency reduces cognitive overhead. When users can quickly assess an AI output's confidence and basis, they spend less mental energy auditing it. Paradoxically, more information about AI uncertainty led to faster action, not more hesitation.
  • Control is non-negotiable. Technical practitioners need to feel like AI assists their judgment, not replaces it. Any workflow where AI could act without visible user acknowledgment created resistance and distrust.
  • Fewer steps, not more. The measure of a good AI feature is whether it removes steps from a workflow. AI features that required users to do extra work to verify, dismiss, or override output were experienced as negative even when the underlying model was accurate.
  • Provenance matters most at decision points. Users tolerated low-transparency AI for low-stakes suggestions. For investigation-critical output — "this is likely a false positive," "this indicates lateral movement" — they demanded drill-down into the underlying data.
  • Consistency builds calibration. When AI behaved consistently across the platform, users developed better calibration for when to trust it. Inconsistency across products meant users had to recalibrate constantly — and calibration requires effort.

The Framework: Six Principles + Twelve Patterns

The AI Experience Standard is built on two layers: foundational principles that guide every AI design decision, and specific patterns that implement those principles in concrete UI interactions.

Six Responsible AI Principles

🔎
Transparency
AI must always be clearly identified. Users should never be uncertain whether an output is AI-generated.
📋
Provenance
AI outputs must be traceable to their underlying data. Users can drill into what drove a recommendation.
🎲
Control
Users decide when and how AI is applied. AI never acts without visible acknowledgment.
⚡
Value
AI must measurably reduce work. Features that add steps, even if technically impressive, don't ship.
🛡
Safety
In high-stakes contexts, AI surfaces recommendations — humans make decisions. Appropriate caution is designed in.
👤
Privacy
Users understand what data AI uses and how. Consent and data handling are visible, not buried.

Twelve AI Workflow Patterns

The 12 patterns cover the full lifecycle of AI interaction for technical practitioners. These are organized into three groups, each addressing a distinct phase of the human-AI workflow:

Surface & Attribution
AI Attribution Badge
Consistent visual treatment for AI-generated content. Users always know what's AI vs. human. Includes icon, label, and expandable context.
✓ Developer observability: AI-generated anomaly flags
Confidence Surfacing
Progressive confidence indicators that communicate model certainty at the right level of detail for the context — from simple low/medium/high to percentage ranges for expert users.
✓ Infrastructure: anomaly detection confidence bands
AI Summary Card
Structured format for AI-generated text summaries. Includes attribution, confidence, source data link, timestamp, and dismiss/correct actions. Never a raw text block.
✓ Log analysis: automated incident summaries
Inline Suggestion
Contextual AI recommendations surfaced inline within a workflow, with clear accept/dismiss/ignore affordances that don't interrupt the flow of investigation.
✓ Query builder: AI-suggested filter refinements
Provenance & Explainability
Explainability Drawer
On-demand panel that shows the reasoning chain behind an AI recommendation: what data was analyzed, what pattern triggered the output, what factors were weighted. Designed for expert users who need to verify before acting.
✓ Root cause analysis: "why did you flag this?"
Source Data Link
Every AI output links directly to the underlying data that generated it. Users can drill into the evidence in one click. Closes the loop between AI summary and raw telemetry.
✓ Metrics: trace from AI flag to raw signal
Progressive Disclosure for AI
AI outputs default to summary level. Users request more detail on demand. Prevents information overload while preserving expert access to full reasoning. Respects that most users won't need the full explainability path.
✓ Alert triage: summary first, depth on demand
AI-Assisted Query Builder
Natural language query input that generates structured filter/query output, with the generated query always visible and editable. AI writes the query; the engineer owns it.
✓ Log/trace search: NL to structured query
Control, Feedback & Safety
Correct & Dismiss Flows
Structured patterns for users to flag incorrect AI output, correct it, or dismiss it. Feedback flows to model improvement. Closes the human-in-the-loop cycle without requiring a separate feedback tool.
✓ Any AI surface with incorrect output
AI Permissions & Controls
Three-level permission model: global AI toggle (admin), product-level AI settings (manager), workspace-level AI preferences (individual). Users control AI exposure at the right level of granularity.
✓ Enterprise platforms: org-level AI governance
Paved Path AI Default
AI-assisted workflows are designed with smart defaults that work for the majority of users without configuration, while expert controls are available for those who need them. The AI path should be the easy path.
✓ Developer tools: AI-assisted by default, configurable
AI Loading & Error States
Consistent treatment for AI processing states, model timeouts, low-confidence outputs, and graceful degradation to non-AI fallback. AI absence should be as well-designed as AI presence.
✓ Any AI feature: resilient experience design

The Tradeoff We Accepted

What We Accepted

We deliberately required every AI pattern to include a "dismiss or correct" path, even when product teams argued their AI feature was accurate enough not to need one. This added design and engineering overhead to every AI feature shipped.

Why

In a high-stakes domain, user trust is more valuable than feature velocity. An AI feature users don't trust because they can't control it is worse than no AI feature. Building correction into every pattern, even at the cost of shipping speed, was a deliberate bet that long-term trust compounds. It did.

Operationalizing the Standard

Patterns in a document don't change products. I worked to make this standard unavoidable — integrated into the tools and processes designers and engineers already use:

  • Design system integration: All 12 patterns were codified as components in the R7 Design System, with Figma libraries that made the compliant pattern the lowest-friction choice.
  • Supernova & MCP integration: The AI standard was ingested into Supernova and connected via MCP, enabling AI copilots to surface pattern guidance in context as designers worked — a recursive loop where AI tooling helped enforce AI UX standards.
  • Design review integration: AI pattern compliance became part of the Design Forum review checklist. Designs with AI features that didn't meet the standard couldn't advance without explicit exception and rationale.
  • Engineer documentation: Every pattern included implementation specs, interaction logic, accessibility requirements, and code guidance. Engineers had everything they needed to implement correctly without a designer in the room.
